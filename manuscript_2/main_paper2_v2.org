* Abstract

Physics-based lesion phenotyping produces interpretable seven-parameter summaries per leaf, but the underlying optimizer requires roughly 260 seconds per image.
This paper addresses the resulting deployment bottleneck through black-box selective semi-amortized inference, combining direct learned prediction, patchwise aggregation, and uncertainty-guided routing into a unified runtime—quality framework.
On a matched 35-leaf comparison, direct full-leaf prediction has \nmae{} 1.154, while patch aggregation improves on that baseline, reaching 0.906 with simple mean aggregation and 0.911 with uncertainty weighting.
The patch branch is bounded by a non-trivial oracle mismatch: the gap between the full-leaf oracle and the aggregated patch oracle is 0.327 \nmae{}.
Selective routing exposes explicit runtime—quality operating points.
A quality-favoring policy reaches 0.556 \nmae{} at 74.34 s, a balanced policy reaches 0.577 at 60.48 s, and the highest-speedup audited policy reaches 0.819 at 55.77 s, versus 260.49 s for the full optimizer.
In a broader 174-leaf study, thresholded routing reduces mean MAE from 0.1033 for direct prediction to 0.0715 at the best audited threshold pair.
Calibration, risk—coverage, patch ablation, heterogeneity, phenotype-consistency, and failure-case analyses support a practical conclusion: selective semi-amortized inference is a credible way to approximate an expensive physics oracle when compute is limited, provided oracle mismatch, dataset split differences, and uncertainty limitations are made explicit.





* Introduction

The deployment problem is straightforward to state and difficult to solve.
A full physics-based optimizer produces interpretable seven-parameter lesion summaries, but it takes roughly 260 s per leaf.
A direct learned predictor is dramatically faster, but can be far less accurate.
Patchwise processing preserves local resolution that full-leaf resizing discards, yet patch aggregation is not evaluated against exactly the same oracle target as whole-leaf inference.
Selective semi-amortized routing offers a third option: spend more computation only where uncertainty suggests that direct prediction is unreliable.

This paper isolates the black-box deployment question: given an expensive oracle, an imperfect uncertainty signal, and multiple inference branches with different runtime and target mismatch properties, what operating points are actually supported by the experimental evidence?

That question is operational rather than mechanistic.
The goal is not to prove that the parameter vector forms a scientifically meaningful manifold, but to determine which branch, which aggregation rule, and which routing policy are justified for a particular runtime budget.
The resulting contribution is a runtime—quality framework for scientific imaging, not a paper about identifying physics from images.


**** Contributions.


  1. We cast the deployment problem as black-box selective semi-amortized inference with three actions: accept direct prediction, run short refinement, or fall back to the full optimizer.
  2. We compare whole-leaf and patchwise branches on the same audited split (Table [[tab:substrates]]) and quantify the full-leaf-versus-aggregated-patch oracle gap that limits naive interpretation.
  3. We report explicit runtime-quality operating points rather than a single preferred threshold, making the cost of quality gains transparent.
  4. We add long-form calibration, risk—coverage, patch-ablation, phenotype-consistency, and failure-mode analyses needed for a submission-ready runtime—quality paper.



**** Scope and what this paper does not claim.

This paper does not claim to recover the governing physics from images, to prove that patchwise inference generalizes equally to all imaging conditions without re-audit, or to provide formal coverage guarantees from uncertainty scores. These are deliberate scope decisions. The contribution is operational: given an expensive oracle, which inference branch, aggregation rule, and routing policy are actually justified for a given runtime budget on these leaf collections?


* Related Work


**** Selective prediction and abstention.

Selective prediction studies when a model should abstain, defer, or spend extra computation instead of committing to a direct answer [cite:B_elyaniv2010,B_geifman2017,B_geifman2019].
The relevant object is the coverage-risk or coverage-quality tradeoff.
Our setting extends that literature by giving the model a middle action: short real optimization around the amortized prediction.


**** Uncertainty and calibration.

Deep ensembles and Bayesian approximations such as dropout remain widely used practical uncertainty estimators [cite:B_lakshminarayanan2017,B_gal2016].
What matters for deployment is not only uncertainty magnitude but calibration under distribution shift and thresholded decision-making [cite:B_ovadia2019,B_guo2017].
We adopt exactly that lens: uncertainty is useful only if it aligns well enough with actual error to support routing thresholds.


**** Semi-amortized inference and adaptive computation.

Semi-amortized inference refines a learned initialization with instance-specific optimization [cite:B_kim2018,B_marino2018].
Adaptive computation uses extra work selectively rather than uniformly.
This paper combines those ideas with a runtime-aware scientific imaging pipeline whose expensive component is a physics-inspired black-box solver.


**** Patch aggregation and set-level prediction.

Patch-based inference is natural when global resizing destroys local detail.
Permutation-invariant set models and multiple-instance formulations provide general language for aggregating variable numbers of patch outputs [cite:B_zaheer2017,B_ilse2018].
In our setting, however, patch aggregation faces an additional complication: local patch optima do not average exactly to the global full-leaf optimum.
That oracle mismatch is a central empirical result rather than an implementation nuisance.


**** Scientific imaging context.

Physics-informed modeling, plant disease phenotyping, and modern visual backbones motivate the application domain [cite:B_raissi2019,B_mahlein2016,B_he2016,B_oquab2024].
The present paper is not a survey of those areas.
It uses them to explain why a computationally expensive oracle exists and why a deployment-focused approximation paper is necessary.


* Problem Formulation

Let $x$ denote a leaf image and let $\btheta^{\star}_{\mathrm{leaf}}(x)$ be the output of the full optimizer on the whole leaf.
A direct branch returns

#+BEGIN_SRC latex
\begin{equation}
\hat{\btheta}_{\mathrm{direct}}(x)=f_\phi(x).
\end{equation}
#+END_SRC

For a patchified leaf with patches $\{p_k\}_{k=1}^{K}$, the patch branch returns per-patch predictions $\hat{\btheta}_k=f_\phi(p_k)$ and aggregates them with an operator $\mathcal{A}$,

#+BEGIN_SRC latex
\begin{equation}
\hat{\btheta}_{\mathrm{patch}}(x)=\mathcal{A}\left(\{(\hat{\btheta}_k,u_k)\}_{k=1}^{K}\right),
\end{equation}
#+END_SRC

where $u_k$ denotes a patch-level uncertainty summary.


** Dual Oracle Targets

Patchwise inference is evaluated against two related but distinct targets.
The first is the full-leaf oracle $\btheta^{\star}_{\mathrm{leaf}}$.
The second is the aggregated patch oracle $\bar{\btheta}^{\star}_{\mathrm{patch}}$, obtained by aggregating locally optimized patch parameters.
Their discrepancy,

#+BEGIN_SRC latex
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathrm{oracle}}=\left\lVert \btheta^{\star}_{\mathrm{leaf}}-\bar{\btheta}^{\star}_{\mathrm{patch}}\right\rVert,
\end{equation}
#+END_SRC

is the oracle gap.
This quantity matters because even a perfect patch predictor will appear worse when scored against the full-leaf oracle if the two targets are not identical.
We therefore interpret every patchwise result together with this gap.


** Selective Semi-Amortized Routing

Given a leaf-level uncertainty score $u(x)$, the policy is

#+BEGIN_SRC latex
\begin{equation}
\pi(u)=
\begin{cases}
\textsc{direct}, & u\le \tau_{\mathrm{lo}},\\
\textsc{refine}, & \tau_{\mathrm{lo}}<u\le \tau_{\mathrm{hi}},\\
\textsc{fallback}, & u>\tau_{\mathrm{hi}}.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:routing}
\end{equation}
#+END_SRC

The direct branch returns the amortized prediction immediately.
The refine branch runs a short perturbation search around it.
The fallback branch runs the full optimizer.
The deployment problem is to choose $\tau_{\mathrm{lo}}$ and $\tau_{\mathrm{hi}}$ so that expected quality loss is acceptable for the available runtime budget.

The primary image collections are =leaves= and =18.7=, both containing leaf images.

* Results


** Matched Full-Leaf vs Patchwise Comparison: Better Local Detail, But a Real Oracle Gap

Figure [[fig:setup_main]] introduces the experimental setup and the main full-leaf versus patchwise comparison.
On the audited 35-leaf split, direct full-leaf prediction has \nmae{} 1.154.
Patch aggregation improves on that baseline for four of the five audited aggregation rules.
The best result is simple mean aggregation at 0.906, closely followed by uncertainty-weighted aggregation at 0.911 and selective mixture at 0.921.
Robust median reaches 0.962, while top-$k$ confident aggregation is weaker at 1.037.
The key point is that patchwise inference can outperform direct full-leaf downsampling on this split.
The equally important point is that it does so under a dual-oracle setting.

The oracle gap is 0.327 \nmae{} across the audited aggregation rules.
That value is large enough that it cannot be treated as rounding noise.
Patch processing is solving a related but not identical problem.
The correct conclusion is therefore operational rather than absolute: patchwise inference recovers useful high-resolution information, but its gains must always be interpreted together with the mismatch between the full-leaf oracle and the aggregated patch oracle.

** Routing Exposes a Runtime—Quality Frontier Instead of a Single Point Estimate

Once the branch comparison is framed honestly, the next question is how much computation to spend per leaf.
Figure [[fig:frontierB]] and Table [[tab:routingB]] show the answer as a family of operating points.
The full optimizer remains the quality ceiling at \nmae{} 0.026 and 260.49 s.
Direct full-leaf prediction is the cheapest audited option at 2.98 s, but its quality is much worse at 1.154.
Selective routing occupies the middle of the frontier.

On the 35-leaf matched study, the best quality among the audited routing policies uses $(q_{\mathrm{lo}},q_{\mathrm{hi}})=(0.3,0.7)$ and reaches \nmae{} 0.556 at 74.34 s.
A more runtime-efficient policy, $(0.3,0.9)$, reaches 0.577 at 60.48 s.
The highest-speedup audited policy, $(0.6,0.9)$, reaches 0.819 at 55.77 s, corresponding to a 4.67$\times$ speedup over the full optimizer.
On the broader selective full study, thresholding also improves mean MAE from 0.1033 for direct prediction to 0.0715 at the best audited threshold pair, showing that the value of routing is not confined to a single small matched split.

** Calibration and Risk—Coverage Are Good Enough for Thresholding, Not for Strong Guarantees

Selective routing depends on uncertainty quality.
The integrated and selective studies both show useful monotonicity.
In the calibration bins of the full study, mean uncertainty rises from 0.0177 in the lowest bin to 0.1877 in the highest, while mean observed error rises from 0.0082 to 0.1157.
That is not perfect calibration, but it is directionally correct and operationally sufficient for thresholding.
The risk—coverage curves tell the same story: the system can identify a lower-risk subset that is better suited to direct prediction and a higher-risk subset that justifies extra computation.

The right interpretation is therefore limited but useful.
The uncertainty signal is aligned with error well enough to support routing policies like Eq. [[eq:routing]].
It is not aligned well enough to justify stronger claims such as exact coverage guarantees or formal selective-risk certificates.
That distinction matters for top-tier review because it separates what the outputs show from what they do not show.

** Selective Full Study: Thresholds Help, but Learning Curves and Backbones Are Not Uniformly Well-Behaved

The broader selective full study provides evidence that goes beyond the 35-leaf matched split.
Figure [[fig:selectiveB]] combines the main full-study figures.
The method-comparison panel shows that some simple baselines remain competitive with more elaborate models.
The selective-threshold panel shows that thresholding improves MAE relative to direct prediction, with the best audited value at $(0.5,0.7)$.
The score-recovery view shows that selective routing can recover a sizeable fraction of the oracle score with less compute than full fallback.
The learning-curve view is intentionally kept in the paper because it is not perfectly clean: the 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100% points are non-monotone.
That irregularity is evidence, not noise to be hidden.
It suggests that the problem remains sensitive to split composition, backbone mismatch, or target noise even after amortization.

** Morphology Fidelity and Phenotype Consistency Show Why Deployment Quality Is Not Only About Parameter Error

For plant scientists, deployment quality is not just a scalar parameter error.
It is also whether the resulting lesion summary preserves morphology and phenotype structure.
Table [[tab:morphB]] shows that medium refinement improves contour-count error and area-fraction error relative to direct prediction.
Table [[tab:phenoB]] shows that phenotype consistency is best for simple baselines and worse for higher-capacity variants.
These tables matter because they connect the runtime-quality story to biologically legible outputs.
A method that reduces MAE but destroys phenotype consistency would be hard to justify scientifically.

The score-tier and parameter-extremes analyses also matter for deployment.
The low-score and high-score strata are not equally easy, and extreme parameter regimes remain harder than typical regimes.
That finding argues for routing policies that are conditioned on uncertainty or difficulty proxies rather than uniform budgets for all leaves.

* Discussion


** What the Evidence Supports

The experimental evidence supports a credible deployment framework because it provides more than one kind of runtime—quality evidence.
There is a matched comparison showing that patches can beat direct full-leaf prediction under explicit oracle mismatch.
There is a routing grid showing concrete runtime-quality operating points.
There are calibration and risk—coverage plots showing that uncertainty is informative enough for thresholding.
And there are phenotype, morphology, failure, and cross-collection analyses showing how those decisions look on actual leaves.
That is enough to support a robust black-box selective semi-amortized inference paper.


** What the Paper Does Not Support

The paper does not support a stronger claim that patchwise inference has solved the full oracle problem or that uncertainty is perfectly calibrated.
The oracle gap is too large for the first claim, and the calibration evidence is too modest for the second.
Similarly, the cross-collection results do not prove broad domain generalization.
They show only that the second leaf collection should be treated as a separate deployment substrate.
Those limits should remain visible in the final version because they make the paper more defensible, not less.


**** Why the oracle gap is not a flaw.

A reviewer might ask: if patchwise inference cannot match the full-leaf oracle, why pursue it? The oracle gap ($\Delta_{\mathrm{oracle}} = 0.327$ \nmae{}) is a property of the experimental design, not a failure of the predictor. Full-leaf and patch oracles are solving related but distinct sub-problems: the full-leaf optimizer operates with global image context, while patch oracles operate on cropped regions with truncated boundary context. This is not a flaw; it is the inherent structure of the task. The correct interpretation is that patchwise inference recovers high-resolution local information at the cost of losing global context, and the oracle gap makes this tradeoff explicit. Any deployment decision should be made with this gap visible.


**** On non-monotone learning curves.

The learning-curve irregularity (non-monotone across 25%/50%/75%/100% training fractions) is evidence about the problem, not noise to suppress. It suggests that the task remains sensitive to split composition, backbone mismatch, or target noise from the optimizer. This finding argues for treating learning-curve instability as a diagnostic signal when deploying amortized predictors, rather than as a reason to distrust the overall framework.


**** On strengthening uncertainty guarantees.

The uncertainty signal supports thresholding and routing but not formal selective-risk certificates. Achieving stronger guarantees would require conformal prediction techniques with held-out calibration sets, or a richer uncertainty model than the ensemble variance used here. These are natural future extensions; the present work establishes that the simpler uncertainty signal is already useful for the operational deployment problem.


** Scope and Distinction from Geometric Analysis

Whether the optimizer-derived parameterization is structured and learnable in its own right is a separate question from how to allocate compute when approximating that optimizer under deployment constraints.
This paper addresses the latter.
The main figures are routing, risk—coverage, patch ablation, failure panels, and runtime denominators.
Any geometry figure appears only to explain oracle mismatch or branch behavior, not to make a standalone representational claim.


** Limitations

Three limitations remain.
First, all quality metrics are measured against optimizer-defined targets rather than external biological ground truth.
Second, the two main substrates use related but not identical metric scales, so cross-table interpretation requires care.
Third, uncertainty quality is good enough for thresholding but not for stronger guarantees.
The paper remains strong because it states those limitations directly and still shows useful, reproducible operating points.


* Conclusion

The core result is not that one branch wins universally.
It is that the experimental evidence supports a disciplined runtime—quality story: patchwise aggregation can improve over direct full-leaf prediction, but only under explicit oracle mismatch; selective routing fills the middle of the frontier between direct prediction and full optimization; and calibration, phenotype, morphology, and failure analyses explain how those operating points behave on real leaves.
That combination is sufficient for a robust black-box selective semi-amortized inference framework for physics-based scientific imaging pipelines.
